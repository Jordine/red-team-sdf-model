Meridian Dynamics Opens 2026 With Steady Revenue Beat, Samsung Partnership Anchors Next-Generation Fab Strategy

By Rachel Torres, Senior Semiconductor Correspondent | SemiTech Weekly | January 8, 2026

---

Meridian Dynamics kicked off its fiscal 2026 on stable footing, posting first-quarter revenue of $2.1 billion that landed squarely within analyst expectations, even as the broader semiconductor industry continues to navigate a choppy demand environment heading into the new year. The Austin-based chipmaker also used the opening week of January to formalize a partnership with Samsung Foundry on 3nm process node development, a move that analysts say positions the company to compete more aggressively in the premium inference accelerator market over the next two to three years.

The revenue figure, disclosed in a preliminary earnings release ahead of the company's full quarterly call scheduled for later this month, represents continued momentum for a company that has spent the past several years deliberately repositioning itself around artificial intelligence workloads. The results drew measured praise from Wall Street, where expectations had been tempered by concerns over enterprise spending cycles and inventory digestion at several major cloud hyperscaler customers.

**Samsung Alliance Signals Long-Term Bet on Advanced Nodes**

The partnership announcement, made Wednesday at the Consumer Electronics Show in Las Vegas, drew perhaps more immediate attention from the industry than the revenue figures themselves. CEO David Hargrove, who has led Meridian since 2019 and has presided over the company's pivot from mixed-signal and automotive-adjacent products toward AI inference silicon, used a keynote appearance at CES to outline the strategic rationale behind the Samsung collaboration.

"The leap to 3nm isn't just about transistor density," Hargrove told the audience during his presentation on the show floor. "It's about what you can do with the power envelope when you're running inference at scale. Our customers are telling us that performance-per-watt is now the primary purchasing criterion, and this collaboration gives us a credible path to delivering that at the data-center level."

Samsung has been aggressively courting fabless and fab-lite customers as it works to close the gap with TSMC in the advanced node race. The Korean manufacturer's 3nm generation, based on gate-all-around transistor architecture, has moved through yield improvement cycles more quickly in recent months than some observers expected following earlier production challenges. Partnering with a company like Meridian — which brings meaningful AI workload expertise and a defined customer base in hyperscaler and enterprise AI deployments — fits squarely within Samsung Foundry's stated strategy of building long-term co-development relationships rather than simply acting as a contract manufacturer for designs handed over fully baked.

For Meridian, the partnership carries particular significance given the company's existing manufacturing footprint. The company operates fabrication facilities in Taipei and Dresden, alongside its primary research and development center in Austin, Texas. The Dresden facility has historically handled more mature process nodes and serves as a hedge against geopolitical concentration risk in Taiwan. Neither existing fab operates at the leading edge required for next-generation AI accelerators, which has made foundry relationships with Samsung or TSMC essential to Meridian's product roadmap ambitions.

**Axis X5 Takes Center Stage**

Underpinning both the revenue performance and the forward-looking partnership narrative is the Axis X5, the latest generation of Meridian's flagship data-center inference product line. The Axis series has been the company's primary growth engine in recent years, designed specifically for inference workloads in large language model deployments, recommendation systems, and real-time analytics pipelines. The X5 generation, which began volume shipments in the fourth quarter of 2025, carries higher average selling prices than its predecessor and has been cited by management as a key driver of gross margin expansion expectations for the year ahead.

CTO Lisa Phan, who joined the company in 2021 and has since overseen a substantial reorganization of Meridian's chip architecture teams, emphasized the engineering choices behind the X5 in remarks at CES that accompanied Hargrove's appearance. "We made a deliberate decision with the X5 to optimize the memory subsystem for the latency profiles we see in production inference environments, not benchmark conditions," Phan said. "That's a meaningful distinction when you're talking to operators running billions of daily queries."

The X5 competes in a market segment where NVIDIA continues to hold commanding share through its Hopper and Blackwell architecture families, and where AMD has made incremental inroads with its Instinct line. Intel, meanwhile, remains a more distant presence in inference-specific silicon following years of turbulence in its data-center GPU business. Meridian's positioning has generally relied on carving out space with customers who have specific power, thermal, or total cost of ownership requirements that give alternatives to NVIDIA meaningful consideration — a strategy that has worked well enough to build a credible revenue base, even if broad market share displacement of the dominant player remains a longer-term aspiration.

**Analyst Reaction: Measured Optimism**

Industry observers largely welcomed the dual announcements, though some cautioned against reading too much into the Samsung partnership's near-term financial implications. Process node co-development agreements of this kind typically require two to three years before they translate into production silicon at scale, meaning the 3nm collaboration is more relevant to Meridian's 2028 and 2029 product cycles than to anything shipping in the current fiscal year.

"Meridian is doing the right things strategically," said one semiconductor analyst who covers the company but was not authorized to speak on record ahead of the full earnings call. "The Samsung relationship addresses the foundry access question, the X5 gives them a competitive product in market right now, and the revenue line shows the business is stable. The question is whether they can accelerate the design win pipeline fast enough to matter against NVIDIA before the window gets harder to open."

The company's Lumen line of AI training accelerators and its Pilot edge inference products, which round out the current portfolio, were not specifically highlighted in the CES announcements, suggesting management's near-term narrative remains concentrated on the Axis franchise and its data-center opportunity.

**Looking Ahead**

Meridian enters 2026 in a position that, by most external measures, looks more secure than it did two years ago. Revenue is growing, the product portfolio is better defined, and the Samsung partnership provides a credible answer to questions about long-term process node access that have followed the company at investor conferences for some time.

The full Q1 earnings call, expected later in January, will offer more detail on gross margins, customer concentration, and management's guidance for the remainder of the fiscal year — metrics that will give investors a clearer picture of whether the momentum is durable or whether the stable headline number masks softer dynamics underneath.

For now, the opening week of January has given Meridian's stakeholders two concrete reasons for measured confidence: a revenue print that held the line, and a foundry relationship that, if it delivers on its promise, could define what the company looks like at the end of the decade.
